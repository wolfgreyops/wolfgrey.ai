Chapter 1

The CRAFT Prompting Framework

Stop writing vague prompts that get vague results. CRAFT gives you a repeatable system for getting exactly what you need from any AI tool.

C

Context

Give the AI background information it needs to understand your situation. The more relevant context, the better the output.

Example: "I run a 10-person marketing agency focused on B2B SaaS companies. We've been in business for 3 years and are trying to expand into enterprise clients."
R

Role

Tell the AI who to be. A specific role shapes how it thinks, what expertise it draws on, and how it communicates.

Example: "Act as a senior sales strategist who has helped 50+ agencies break into enterprise sales. You're direct, practical, and focused on actionable advice."
A

Action

Be specific about what you want done. Vague requests get vague results. Tell the AI exactly what task to perform.

Example: "Create a cold outreach email sequence (3 emails) for reaching enterprise marketing directors. Each email should be under 150 words."
F

Format

Define how you want the output structured. Tables, bullet points, numbered lists, specific sections—be explicit.

Example: "Format each email with: Subject line, Body text, Suggested send timing, Follow-up notes. Use markdown formatting."
T

Tone

Set the voice and style. Professional? Casual? Technical? The tone shapes whether the output sounds like you.

Example: "Write in a confident but not arrogant tone. Sound like a peer, not a salesperson. Avoid jargon and buzzwords."
Chapter 2

40+ Ready-to-Use Templates

Copy, customize, and use. Each template is built on the CRAFT framework and designed for real business tasks.

Cold Outreach Sequence

Sales

3-email sequence for cold prospects. Builds curiosity without being salesy.

Context: I run [BUSINESS TYPE] serving [TARGET MARKET]. Our main value proposition is [VALUE PROP]. Average deal size is [AMOUNT]. Role: Act as a cold outreach specialist who gets 40%+ open rates. You write like a human, not a marketer. Action: Create a 3-email cold outreach sequence for [TARGET TITLE] at [COMPANY TYPE]. - Email 1: Pattern interrupt, create curiosity - Email 2: Social proof, specific result - Email 3: Breakup email, final value offer Format: For each email provide: - Subject line (under 40 chars) - Body (under 100 words) - Send timing recommendation - Follow-up action if no response Tone: Conversational, peer-to-peer. No corporate speak. Sound like you're emailing a colleague, not a prospect.

Discovery Call Questions

Sales

Smart questions that uncover real pain points and buying signals.

Context: I sell [PRODUCT/SERVICE] to [TARGET MARKET]. Typical problems we solve: [LIST 2-3 PROBLEMS]. Sales cycle is usually [TIMEFRAME]. Role: Act as a sales coach who trains SDRs at high-growth startups. You focus on questions that reveal pain, not surface-level discovery. Action: Create 15 discovery call questions organized by stage: - Opening (build rapport, set agenda) - Problem exploration (dig into pain) - Impact questions (quantify the cost of inaction) - Solution fit (understand their requirements) - Next steps (identify decision process) Format: Numbered list with brief notes on what each question reveals and how to follow up based on common answers. Tone: Natural and conversational. These should sound like genuine curiosity, not an interrogation.

Proposal Generator

Sales

Turn discovery notes into a compelling proposal draft.

Context: I provide [SERVICE] for [TARGET MARKET]. My pricing model is [DESCRIBE]. Here are notes from my discovery call with [PROSPECT NAME]: [PASTE YOUR NOTES] Role: Act as a proposal writer who has closed $10M+ in B2B deals. You lead with client problems, not your features. Action: Create a proposal draft that includes: 1. Executive summary (their situation + your solution) 2. Understanding of their challenges (show you listened) 3. Proposed approach (what you'll do, in phases) 4. Investment and timeline 5. Why us (brief differentiators) 6. Next steps Format: Use headers, bullet points, and bold for key terms. Keep total length under 2 pages when formatted. Tone: Confident and client-focused. Talk about them 3x more than you talk about yourself.

Objection Handler

Sales

Responses for the 10 most common sales objections.

Context: I sell [PRODUCT/SERVICE] at [PRICE POINT] to [TARGET MARKET]. Common competitors include [LIST COMPETITORS]. Our main differentiators are [LIST 2-3]. Role: Act as a sales trainer who specializes in objection handling. You never argue with prospects—you acknowledge, reframe, and redirect. Action: Create responses for these objections: 1. "It's too expensive" 2. "We're already using [competitor]" 3. "I need to think about it" 4. "We don't have budget right now" 5. "Send me more information" 6. "I need to talk to my partner/team" 7. "We tried something like this before" 8. "We're too busy right now" 9. "What makes you different?" 10. "Can you do a discount?" Format: For each objection provide: - The underlying concern (what they're really saying) - Response script (2-3 sentences max) - Follow-up question to ask Tone: Empathetic but confident. Never defensive.

Content Calendar

Marketing

30 days of content ideas for any platform, organized by theme.

Context: My business is [WHAT YOU DO] serving [TARGET AUDIENCE]. Key topics we should cover: [LIST 3-5 TOPICS]. Our brand voice is [DESCRIBE]. Primary platform is [PLATFORM]. Role: Act as a content strategist who has grown multiple business accounts to 50K+ followers. You understand what drives engagement AND conversions. Action: Create a 30-day content calendar with: - 4 weekly themes (one per week) - 5 posts per week (Mon-Fri) - Mix of content types: educational (40%), storytelling (30%), promotional (20%), engagement (10%) Format: Table format with columns: Day | Theme | Content Type | Post Topic | Hook/Headline | CTA Tone: Match our brand voice. Every post should feel like it could only come from us.

Case Study Writer

Marketing

Turn client results into compelling case studies.

Context: I want to create a case study about work we did for [CLIENT NAME/INDUSTRY]. Here's the raw information: - Their situation before: [DESCRIBE] - What we did: [DESCRIBE] - Results achieved: [LIST METRICS/OUTCOMES] - Timeline: [HOW LONG] - Quote from client (if available): [QUOTE] Role: Act as a B2B marketing writer who specializes in case studies that close deals. You know that specificity beats generality. Action: Write a case study with these sections: 1. Headline (result-focused, specific) 2. Quick stats (3-4 key metrics, visually scannable) 3. The Challenge (what they were struggling with) 4. The Solution (what we did, briefly) 5. The Results (detailed outcomes with numbers) 6. Key Takeaway (one-liner insight) Format: Use headers, bullet points, and pull quotes. Keep total length under 800 words. Tone: Professional but not boring. Let the results speak loudly.

Email Newsletter

Marketing

Weekly newsletter template that builds trust and drives action.

Context: I send a [FREQUENCY] newsletter to [AUDIENCE SIZE] subscribers. They are [DESCRIBE AUDIENCE]. The newsletter is about [TOPIC AREA]. My goal is [EDUCATE/SELL/BUILD TRUST]. Topic for this issue: [DESCRIBE THE TOPIC] Role: Act as a newsletter writer with 50K+ subscribers. You know that value comes first, and the best newsletters feel like a smart friend sharing insights. Action: Write a newsletter that includes: 1. Subject line (curiosity-driven, under 50 chars) 2. Preview text (complements subject, under 100 chars) 3. Opening hook (personal or provocative, 2-3 sentences) 4. Main content (one big idea, 200-300 words) 5. Actionable takeaway (what they can do today) 6. Soft CTA (related to my business, not pushy) 7. Sign-off Format: Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max). Use line breaks for readability. Include one optional content block idea (link, image, or quote). Tone: [DESCRIBE YOUR VOICE]. Write like you're emailing one person, not a list.

Landing Page Copy

Marketing

High-converting landing page structure and copy.

Context: I need a landing page for [PRODUCT/SERVICE/OFFER]. Price point: [AMOUNT]. Target audience: [WHO]. The main problem we solve: [PROBLEM]. Key benefit: [BENEFIT]. Role: Act as a conversion copywriter who has written pages that generate 6-figures in sales. You follow the PAS framework (Problem-Agitate-Solution) and know that clarity beats cleverness. Action: Write landing page copy with these sections: 1. Hero: Headline (benefit-focused) + Subheadline + CTA button text 2. Problem: 3 pain points they're experiencing 3. Agitate: What happens if they don't solve this 4. Solution: Introduce your offer as the answer 5. Benefits: 4-6 benefit blocks (not features) 6. Social proof: Structure for testimonials 7. How it works: 3-step process 8. FAQ: 5 common objections as questions 9. Final CTA: Urgency-driven close Format: Use headers for each section. Write actual copy, not placeholders. Tone: Confident, clear, conversational. No hype or false urgency.

SOP Generator

Operations

Turn any process into a documented, trainable procedure.

Context: I need to document our process for [PROCESS NAME]. This is currently done by [WHO]. It happens [FREQUENCY]. Current steps (rough): [DESCRIBE WHAT HAPPENS]. Role: Act as an operations consultant who has documented processes for companies scaling from 5 to 50 employees. You know that the best SOPs are simple enough for day-one employees. Action: Create a Standard Operating Procedure that includes: 1. Purpose (why this process exists, 1-2 sentences) 2. Scope (what this covers and doesn't cover) 3. Prerequisites (what's needed before starting) 4. Step-by-step procedure (numbered, specific, actionable) 5. Decision points (if X, then Y scenarios) 6. Quality checklist (how to verify it was done right) 7. Common mistakes to avoid 8. Time estimate 9. Owner and review date Format: Use numbered lists for steps. Include sub-steps where needed. Add notes/tips in italic. Tone: Clear and direct. Write for someone doing this for the first time.

Topics to cover: [LIST TOPICS] Role: Act as a meeting facilitator who runs executive offsites. You know that great meetings have clear outcomes, time limits, and assigned owners. Discussion questions for each item 5. Decision points (what needs to be decided) 6. Action items template 7. Parking lot for off-topic items Format: Table format for agenda items with columns: Time | Topic | Owner | Objective Tone: Professional and efficient. This should make meetings feel purposeful.

Weekly Review

Operations

Structured reflection that improves every week.

Context: I want to do a weekly review of my business. Key areas: [LIST: sales, marketing, operations, etc.]. Current goals: [LIST TOP 3 GOALS]. Here's what happened this week: [PASTE YOUR NOTES, METRICS, OR BRAIN DUMP] Role: Act as a business coach who helps founders run $1M+ companies. You focus on patterns, not just events, and always tie reflection to action. Action: Help me complete my weekly review: 1. Wins (what went well and why) 2. Losses (what didn't work and what to learn) 3. Metrics check (are we on track for our goals?) 4. Energy audit (what gave/drained energy) 5. Key insight (the one thing I learned this week) 6. Next week priorities (top 3 non-negotiables) 7. One thing to stop doing 8. One thing to start doing Format: Use my notes to fill in each section. Ask clarifying questions if needed. Tone: Direct and honest. Help me see blind spots.

Competitive Analysis

Strategy

Deep dive into competitors to find your edge.

Context: My business is [WHAT YOU DO] serving [TARGET MARKET]. My main competitors are: [LIST 3-5 COMPETITORS]. My current positioning is [DESCRIBE]. Role: Act as a competitive strategist who advises growth-stage startups. You look for gaps, not just differences, and focus on actionable insights. Action: Conduct a competitive analysis covering: 1. Competitor overview (what each does, who they serve, pricing) 2. Positioning map (how each positions themselves) 3. Strengths and weaknesses (for each competitor) 4. Gap analysis (what none of them do well) 5. Messaging comparison (how they talk about the problem) 6. Channel strategy (where they show up) 7. Our differentiation opportunities 8. Recommended positioning statement Format: Use tables for comparisons. Bullet points for insights. Tone: Analytical but actionable. Every insight should lead to a "so what?"

Pricing Strategy

Strategy

Develop pricing that captures value and drives growth.

Context: I'm reviewing pricing for [PRODUCT/SERVICE]. Current price: [AMOUNT]. Competitor prices: [RANGE]. Our costs: [DESCRIBE]. Target customer: [WHO]. Value we provide: [DESCRIBE]. Role: Act as a pricing consultant who has helped 50+ companies increase revenue through pricing optimization. You understand both the math and the psychology of pricing. Action: Develop a pricing strategy that includes: 1. Value analysis (what is this actually worth to customers?) 2. Competitive positioning (where should we sit vs. competitors?) 3. Pricing model options (hourly, project, retainer, value-based, etc.) 4. Recommended price point with rationale 5. Pricing psychology tactics to apply 6. Objection handling for the new price 7. Grandfather strategy for existing customers 8. A/B testing approach for validation Format: Structured sections with clear recommendations. Include a decision matrix if relevant. Tone: Strategic and confident. Pricing is about value, not cost.

Quarterly Planning

Strategy

Set and structure your next 90 days for maximum impact.

Context: I'm planning Q[NUMBER] for my business. Last quarter results: [SUMMARIZE]. Current revenue: [AMOUNT]. Team size: [NUMBER]. Biggest challenges: [LIST]. Biggest opportunities: [LIST]. Role: Act as a strategic planning facilitator who works with founder-led companies. You believe in focus over volume—fewer priorities, better execution. Action: Help me create a quarterly plan with: 1. Quarter theme (one phrase that captures the focus) 2. 3 big rocks (the must-win battles this quarter) 3. Key metrics (1-2 per big rock, with targets) 4. 90-day milestones (monthly checkpoints) 5. Resource allocation (where to invest time/money) 6. What to say no to (explicitly) 7. Risk assessment (what could derail us) 8. Weekly rhythm (how we stay on track) Format: Start with a summary, then detailed sections for each big rock. Tone: Ambitious but realistic. Great quarters are won through focus.

Decision Framework

Strategy

Make better decisions on complex business choices.

Context: I need to make a decision about [DESCRIBE THE DECISION]. Options I'm considering: [LIST OPTIONS]. Timeline: [WHEN DO I NEED TO DECIDE]. Stakes: [WHAT'S AT RISK]. Relevant information: [PASTE ANY CONTEXT, DATA, OR CONSIDERATIONS] Role: Act as a strategic advisor who helps CEOs make high-stakes decisions. You ask the questions they're not asking themselves and stress-test assumptions. Action: Help me think through this decision: 1. Clarify the real question (am I solving the right problem?) 2. Identify decision criteria (what matters most?) 3. Evaluate each option against criteria 4. Second-order effects (what happens after the decision?) 5. Reversibility check (how hard is this to undo?) 6. Pre-mortem (if this fails, why did it fail?) 7. Gut check (what is my intuition saying?) 8. Recommendation with confidence level Format: Work through each step. Challenge my assumptions. Ask clarifying questions if needed. Tone: Thoughtful and rigorous. Help me think, don't just give me an answer.
